# RavenStack — A Churn & Retention Monitoring Tool for Customer Success

## Executive Summary

This analysis examines why accounts churn on RavenStack — a fictional B2B SaaS company — and what differentiates the customers worth retaining, using a synthetic dataset of 500 accounts observed over 24 months. Because the data is synthetic, the findings demonstrate analytical methodology rather than real commercial outcomes. The central result is that RavenStack's retention problem is **front-loaded into early tenure**: accounts are lost fastest in their very first month, and those that survive it tend to stay. The steepest single drop in the entire customer lifecycle happens between Month 0 and Month 1 (−15.7pp), and by Month 12 fewer than half the original cohort remains. Headline churn looks alarming at first glance — but, as the findings show, that figure is inflated by an end-of-pilot artifact, and the real story is not a broken product. It is a business that loses customers before they ever activate.

A counterintuitive finding sharpens the diagnosis. The standard assumption in churn analysis is that accounts disengage — use the product less — before they cancel. RavenStack's data shows the opposite: over a 60-day pre-churn window, churning accounts were *more* active than retained ones across every usage measure. The real risk signal is not declining engagement but **short tenure combined with usage concentrated relative to that tenure** — new accounts burning through the product quickly, not quietly fading. For the Customer Success team, this relocates the lever entirely: the opportunity is in the activation window, not in win-back campaigns aimed at disengaging accounts.

The analysis addresses the canonical metrics of Revenue & Growth Analytics — logo churn rate, gross revenue retention (GRR), cohort retention, customer lifetime value (LTV), activation, and ideal customer profile (ICP) — and translates each into an operational implication for the team. Its output is a monitoring tool: a Tableau dashboard backed by a reproducible Python pipeline, built for RavenStack's VP of Customer Success to flag at-risk accounts, read retention across cohorts, and locate the segments worth defending. A churn-risk scoring model (logistic regression, AUC 0.813) underpins the dashboard's Risk List, surfacing the 17 highest-risk accounts — 13% of the active base — for immediate intervention.

## Context

RavenStack is a fictional B2B SaaS company that sells software to other businesses on a recurring-subscription basis. Its customers are accounts — companies, not individual users — and a single account typically runs several concurrent subscriptions at once rather than one flat plan. Revenue is therefore the sum of many small, renewable commitments, and the company's financial health is a function of how many of those commitments persist, expand, or lapse over time.

In a subscription business, retention is not one metric among many — it is the one the model rests on. A transactional business books its revenue at the point of sale; a SaaS business books a promise that only pays off if the customer stays. Acquiring an account is expensive, the value of that account accrues gradually over its lifetime, and an account that leaves before it matures never returns the cost of winning it. Growth in new logos can mask decay in the existing base, which is why the durable question for a SaaS company is rarely "how many did we sign?" and almost always "how many did we keep, and which ones?"

The stakeholder responsible for that second question is the VP of Customer Success. The CS team works account by account — a renewal here, an escalation there — but operates without the systemic view that would let it act strategically: which acquisition cohorts are decaying faster than others, which accounts are sliding toward cancellation before they say so, and which customer segments are worth defending hardest. That pattern-level picture exists in the data but is invisible from inside the day-to-day. This analysis exists to surface it.

The raw material for that view is behavioral and financial: product usage events (how often and how broadly an account engages), subscription status and its transitions, recurring revenue per account, and the churn event itself when an account cancels. These signals are what the analysis reads to infer the health of an account before its outcome is final — the leading indicators that a renewal is at risk while there is still time to act on it.

One tension runs underneath the entire analysis and motivates the tool built on top of it: a subscription business can look healthy in dollars while bleeding accounts underneath. Revenue can hold steady — even grow — while the number of surviving customers quietly erodes, because a handful of large accounts can carry the dollar figures while many small ones disappear. Distinguishing dollar health from account survival is precisely the kind of thing a monitoring tool has to make visible, and it is a thread this analysis returns to repeatedly.

## Approach

The analysis was structured around a single central question: *which accounts are at the highest risk of churning, how does retention behave across acquisition cohorts, and which customer segments generate the most lifetime value?* That question decomposed into a series of sub-questions covering monthly and cumulative churn, cohort retention curves, MRR movement, pre-churn behavioral signals, channel- and segment-level LTV, ideal-customer-profile identification, and a predictive risk model — moving deliberately from describing what happened, to explaining why, to flagging which accounts are most likely to churn next.

The dataset is synthetic: 500 accounts observed monthly over a 24-month pilot window (January 2023 to December 2024). Each account carries not a single plan but several concurrent subscriptions — typically eight to ten active at once — so an account's revenue is an aggregate that rarely collapses to zero even when individual subscriptions lapse. This concurrent-subscription architecture is not an incidental detail; it is the structural feature that later distorts dollar-retention metrics, and naming it up front is what makes those distortions interpretable rather than surprising.

The core churn analysis was restricted to non-trial accounts (403 of the 500), since trial accounts that never convert are not retention events in the commercial sense. December 2024 was excluded from monthly churn rates: it coincides with the pilot's shutdown, producing a mass-cancellation artifact that reflects the end of the observation window rather than organic customer behavior. Cumulative figures retain it but flag it explicitly; monthly rates exclude it. Early attrition is deliberately *not* excluded from the denominators — accounts that churn shortly after signup are precisely the population the analysis is most interested in, so removing them would erase the central finding.

Retention and revenue were measured with canonical SaaS metrics, each defined by an explicit denominator and window to keep them unambiguous:

- **Logo churn rate** — the share of accounts that cancel. Reported two ways: a cumulative 67.5% over the full 24-month pilot (272 of 403 non-trial accounts, inflated by the December shutdown), and a monthly rate of roughly 15% excluding that artifact. The two are not interchangeable, and the writeup is explicit about which is in use at any point.
- **Gross revenue retention (GRR)** — dollar retention of an existing revenue base, expansion excluded. Computed as the median of twelve calendar-anchored 12-month rolling windows (Jan–Dec 2023 start months): for each anchor month, the denominator is the total MRR of accounts active that month, and the numerator is those same accounts' MRR twelve months later, capped at their starting MRR so expansion cannot mask losses. The median across the twelve windows is 97.8%.
- **Revenue churn rate** — MRR lost to cancellations, measured at the account level, roughly 6% monthly excluding December.
- **Cohort retention** — survival of accounts grouped by signup month, tracked period over period; this measures *account* survival, not dollars, and falls to 47.1% by Month 12.
- **LTV by acquisition channel** — realized lifetime value per account, segmented by referral source, read alongside each channel's churn rate.

One methodological caution governs how two of these are read together: logo churn (97.8% GRR notwithstanding) and revenue retention rest on different denominators — signup-cohort account survival versus calendar-month dollar base — and are not directly comparable. High dollar retention coexisting with heavy logo attrition is not a contradiction; it is what the concurrent-subscription structure produces, and the Key Findings section treats the two as complementary lenses rather than competing numbers.

That same structure forced a deliberate exclusion. **Net revenue retention (NRR) was dropped from the analysis.** Because accounts accumulate concurrent subscriptions, NRR computed on this dataset inflates to roughly 335% — a figure with no meaningful relationship to the 100–120% range that NRR is designed to communicate in real SaaS reporting. Rather than report a metric the architecture renders uninterpretable, the analysis uses GRR as its primary dollar-retention measure and documents the NRR exclusion as a rigor decision, revisited in Limitations.

The analytical pipeline was implemented in Python (pandas and numpy for processing, matplotlib and seaborn for exploratory visualization), with a churn-risk scoring model trained in scikit-learn to rank accounts by cancellation probability. The model's construction and what it revealed are taken up in Key Findings; here it is enough to say it governs the dashboard's account-prioritization view. The final outputs are a reproducible analysis script, a set of exported metric tables, and a four-view Tableau dashboard — Risk List, Executive Summary, Cohort Retention, and Segment Health — published to Tableau Public for ongoing use by the Customer Success team.

## Key Findings

### The retention problem is front-loaded into the first month

The single most important pattern in the data is *when* accounts leave, not how many. Tracking survival by signup cohort, the steepest drop in the entire customer lifecycle happens immediately: an average of 15.7 percentage points are lost between Month 0 and Month 1 — the largest single-period loss anywhere in the curve. From there, attrition continues but the slope flattens; by Month 12, cohort survival has fallen to 47.1%. The shape matters more than the endpoint. A retention curve that collapses early and then stabilizes describes a very different business problem than one that bleeds steadily throughout — it says the risk is concentrated in a narrow, identifiable window, and that accounts which clear that window tend to stay. Accounts that fail to find their footing in the first month rarely recover later; the cohort matrix shows almost no return path once an account stalls early. For Customer Success, this is the most actionable fact in the analysis: the intervention window is short, early, and well-defined.

### High logo churn and high dollar retention are both true — and not a contradiction

Read in isolation, two of the headline numbers look irreconcilable. Cumulative logo churn over the pilot is 67.5% (272 of 403 non-trial accounts), and even setting aside the December shutdown that inflates it, the monthly logo churn rate runs around 15% — high by any standard. Yet gross revenue retention sits at 97.8%, comfortably above the typical SaaS benchmark of 80–90%. A business cannot be both losing most of its customers and keeping nearly all of its revenue — unless the two figures are measured on different things, which is exactly the case here.

The two metrics rest on different denominators. Logo churn measures *account survival* within a signup cohort, tracked over the cohort's lifetime. GRR measures *dollar retention* on a calendar-month revenue base — the MRR of accounts active in a given month, checked twelve months later. The reconciling mechanism is the concurrent-subscription architecture: because each account runs eight to ten subscriptions at once, an account rarely drops to zero revenue even as individual subscriptions lapse, and the revenue base in any given calendar month is dominated by large, surviving accounts. Dollars are sticky even while logos churn. The same MRR-growth trend confirms it — recurring revenue grew consistently from January 2023 through November 2024 (December excluded as the shutdown artifact) while accounts were disappearing underneath. This is the divergence the Context section flagged, now quantified: the business looks healthy in dollars and troubled in logos, and a monitoring tool has to show both lenses at once rather than letting one mask the other. It also reframes the alarming 67.5%: the product is not failing the customers who stay — it is failing to keep customers long enough to find out.

### Accounts heading for churn don't disengage — they burn hot

The most useful surprise in the analysis was a hypothesis that didn't survive. A standard assumption in churn work is that accounts disengage before they cancel — usage tapers off, logins thin out, and that decline is the early-warning signal. The pre-churn observation window was built specifically to catch that taper. The data contradicted it. Over a 60-day pre-churn window, accounts that went on to cancel were *more* active than retained accounts across every usage measure — 5 active days versus 4, 46 events versus 35, 4 distinct features versus 3 — and the pattern held stable across 30-, 60-, and 90-day windows. Disengagement is not the signal.

The resolution is a matter of normalization. What predicts churn is not usage *volume* but usage *density relative to tenure*: new accounts compressing a lot of activity into a short lifespan. A high-usage account that has been around for two years looks nothing like a high-usage account that signed up six weeks ago, even if their raw activity is identical. The first is engaged; the second is a flight risk — burning through the product fast and early, often the behavior of a customer evaluating intensely and deciding quickly, not settling in. This finding inverts the intuitive intervention. Watching for accounts that go quiet would have missed the actual churners entirely; the accounts worth worrying about were the ones that looked, by volume alone, like the healthiest in the base.

### The risk model turns the signal into a ranked list

That insight is what the churn-risk scoring model operationalizes. A logistic regression model was trained to estimate each account's cancellation probability, and it was chosen deliberately over a higher-variance alternative: it reached an AUC of 0.813, outperforming a gradient-boosted model (XGBoost, AUC 0.764) while remaining fully interpretable — every account's risk score decomposes into named, explainable drivers, which matters more for a Customer Success team that has to justify why it is calling a given customer than a marginal accuracy gain would. The model's two dominant features restate the behavioral finding in predictive terms: usage density normalized by tenure pushes risk *up* (coefficient +0.75), and longer tenure pulls it *down* (−0.74). Short-tenure accounts with concentrated usage are the model's highest-risk profile — the same "burning hot, early" pattern, now scored and rankable.

Performance was measured out-of-sample. The 403 non-trial accounts were split 80/20 with stratification on churn outcome and a fixed random seed — 322 accounts for training, 81 held out for evaluation — and the feature scaler was fit on the training data alone to prevent leakage. The reported AUC of 0.813 reflects performance on that held-out set, not on data the model had already seen. No k-fold cross-validation was performed; with only 81 accounts in the test set, the estimate carries meaningful variance, which is why out-of-time validation is flagged as future work rather than treated as settled.

The output feeds the dashboard's Risk List, which surfaces the 17 highest-risk accounts (13% of the active base) ranked by cancellation probability. Those accounts represent roughly $317K in monthly recurring revenue (≈$3.8M annualized) — 11.3% of the active base's MRR — concentrated, as the model's drivers predict, in recently-signed accounts.

### Where to grow: the ICP hides in the channel data

Once early churn is contained, the strategic question turns to which accounts are worth acquiring more of — and the segment data points to a clear ideal customer profile. Enterprise accounts in FinTech and HealthTech acquired through the Ads channel show both the lowest churn (57.7%) and a top-tier average LTV (~$108K): the segment that stays longest and is worth the most. That is the profile to defend and to replicate in acquisition.

The channel breakdown underneath that conclusion carries a counterintuitive detail. Conventional SaaS expectation holds that organic and inbound customers — who arrive already convinced — retain better than paid-acquired ones. RavenStack's data runs the other way. The Organic channel produces the highest realized LTV of any source ($108,291) but also the worst retention (73.4% churn), while Ads pairs near-identical LTV ($106,549) with markedly better retention (57.7% churn). Organic accounts, in other words, are valuable when they stay but leave at a high rate — a signal of strong initial fit paired with weaker activation or onboarding follow-through, which ties directly back to the Month 1 problem. Ads is the channel that best combines value and durability. The takeaway is not "spend less on organic" but "organic accounts need the activation support that paid accounts are evidently getting" — a retention fix, not an acquisition cut.

## The Tool in Action

The findings above describe RavenStack as a system. The dashboard built on top of them is what makes the system operable — it lets the VP of Customer Success move from a pattern in the aggregate to a decision about a specific account or segment in a single session. Three views carry the weight, each mapping to one part of the central question.

### Risk List — who needs a call this week

The Risk List answers the first question the CS team actually asks on a Monday morning: of every active account, which ones should I contact before they cancel? The view ranks accounts by the model's cancellation probability and surfaces the 17 flagged as high-risk — 13% of the active base — at the top. Crucially, each account's score is not a black box: it decomposes into the named drivers behind it, so a high-risk flag reads as "short tenure, usage concentrated well above what its age would predict" rather than an opaque number. That distinction is what makes the list usable. A CS manager can open it, see *why* an account is at risk, and walk into the conversation with a specific hypothesis instead of a generic check-in. The view turns the behavioral finding — that the dangerous accounts are the young, hot-burning ones, not the quiet ones — into a worklist sorted by urgency.

### Cohort Retention — confirming the problem is structural, not a bad month

The Cohort Retention view renders survival as a matrix: signup cohorts down the rows, months since signup across the columns, each cell shaded by the share of the cohort still active. The dominant visual feature is a sharp drop between the first two columns — the Month 0 → Month 1 cliff — that repeats across nearly every cohort rather than appearing in one unlucky group. That consistency is the insight the view exists to deliver. Without it, a VP might reasonably suspect that a particular cohort was poorly onboarded, or that one quarter's intake was unusually weak, and chase the wrong fix. The matrix shows the loss is systemic and time-located: it happens to everyone, and it happens immediately. That single visual is the difference between treating early churn as a recurring operational fire and recognizing it as a structural gap in the activation window — which is the case for investing in onboarding rather than in cohort-by-cohort damage control.

### Segment Health — where to defend and where to grow

The Segment Health view crosses customer segment against acquisition channel and shades each cell by retention and realized LTV, turning the ICP question into something the VP can read off a grid. The standout cell is Enterprise accounts in FinTech and HealthTech acquired through Ads: the lowest churn (57.7%) paired with the highest average LTV (~$108K) — the segment to protect first and to acquire more of. The same view exposes the channel tension that raw LTV alone would hide. Organic shows the highest LTV of any source but among the worst retention, while Ads pairs nearly the same LTV with markedly better durability. Seeing value and retention side by side in one grid is what stops the team from over-investing in a channel that looks lucrative on LTV but quietly leaks accounts — and points instead to the real lever for Organic, which is activation support, not acquisition spend. Used together, the three views move the team from diagnosis to action: who to call now, why the problem keeps happening, and which customers are worth the most effort.

## Recommendations

Each recommendation below translates a specific finding into an operational decision for the Customer Success team, ordered to follow the analysis: contain early churn first, operate the risk signal, then turn to growth.

**Make Month 1 activation the team's primary retention metric.** The single largest loss in the customer lifecycle happens between Month 0 and Month 1 (−15.7pp), and accounts that stall in that window rarely recover. Retention dashboards and CS targets that center on later-stage renewals are watching the wrong part of the curve. The metric that deserves the most prominent position — in the weekly review, in team goals, in any account-health summary — is whether a new account reaches activation within its first month. Everything downstream depends on it.

**Build a structured first-30-days activation playbook.** Making activation the metric is only useful if there is a defined motion behind it. Establish an explicit early-tenure protocol: a named activation event to drive each new account toward, a contact cadence triggered on signup rather than on a renewal date, and an escalation when an account approaches the end of its first month without activating. The cohort matrix shows the loss is structural and repeats across nearly every cohort — which means it is addressable by a standard process, not by ad-hoc rescue of individual accounts.

**Re-score the risk list weekly and assign each high-risk account a named owner.** The churn-risk model (AUC 0.813) produces a ranked, interpretable list of accounts by cancellation probability; the 17 currently flagged as high-risk are 13% of the active base and represent ≈$3.8M in annualized recurring revenue. Operationalize it as a recurring worklist: regenerate the scores on a fixed cadence, route the top accounts to specific CS owners rather than a shared queue, and use each account's score decomposition to brief the owner on *why* it is at risk before they reach out. The model's value is realized only if its output becomes someone's Monday-morning queue.

**Watch usage density relative to tenure, not usage decline, as the early-warning signal.** The intuitive churn signal — accounts going quiet before they cancel — was not present in the data; churning accounts were *more* active than retained ones. Monitoring for disengagement would systematically miss the actual flight risks. Reconfigure the early-warning logic to flag the real pattern instead: new accounts compressing high activity into a short lifespan. The model already encodes this (usage density up-weights risk, tenure down-weights it); the team's manual heuristics and any alerting rules should be re-pointed at the same signal.

**Close Organic's activation gap rather than cutting Organic spend.** Organic produces the highest realized LTV of any channel ($108,291) but also the worst retention (73.4% churn) — strong initial fit undermined by weaker activation follow-through, the Month 1 problem showing up channel-specifically. The reflex to pull budget from a high-churn channel would be a mistake here: the accounts are valuable when they stay. Direct the new activation playbook at Organic accounts first, where the gap between LTV potential and realized retention is widest, and measure whether their Month 1 survival closes toward the Ads channel's.

**Acquire more of the proven ICP: Enterprise FinTech/HealthTech via Ads.** Once early churn is contained, this is where growth compounds. Enterprise accounts in FinTech and HealthTech acquired through Ads show the lowest churn (57.7%) and the highest average LTV (~$108K) in the dataset — the segment that stays longest and is worth the most. Feed that profile back to the acquisition side as the target to weight toward, and use the Segment Health view to monitor whether incoming cohorts increasingly match it.

## Limitations & Future Work

### Limitations

**Synthetic data.** The dataset is synthetic, and that is the load-bearing caveat for everything above. The findings demonstrate analytical methodology — how to decompose churn, reconcile divergent retention metrics, build an interpretable risk model — rather than the behavior of a real company. The patterns are internally consistent, but they have not been validated against a live business, and every threshold and benchmark used here would need recalibration against real subscription data before it could drive actual decisions. The transferable asset is the approach, not the specific numbers.

**The concurrent-subscription architecture distorts dollar metrics — and forced the exclusion of NRR.** Each account runs eight to ten subscriptions at once, which means an account's revenue almost never collapses to zero even as individual subscriptions lapse. This structural feature is what keeps GRR high (97.8%) in a way that is not directly comparable to real-SaaS GRR, and it is why GRR is reported only alongside its denominator and its caveat rather than as a clean health number. Its most extreme consequence is net revenue retention: computed on this dataset, NRR inflates to roughly 335%, a figure with no meaningful relationship to the 100–120% range NRR is designed to communicate. NRR was therefore excluded entirely. In a dataset with realistic single-subscription-per-account structure, NRR would be the primary retention metric; here, reporting it would have been worse than omitting it, and the exclusion is a deliberate rigor decision rather than a gap.

**The December 2024 shutdown artifact.** The pilot's observation window ends in a mass cancellation that reflects the experiment closing, not customer behavior. This artifact inflates any metric computed over the full period — most visibly the 67.5% cumulative logo churn. It is mitigated by reporting monthly rates excluding December (around 15%), but it remains a contaminant in lifetime-cumulative figures, and those figures should be read with the artifact in mind rather than at face value.

**Association, not causation — and the limits of the model.** The relationships reported here — usage density and churn, channel and retention, tenure and risk — are statistical associations measured on observational data, not proven causal mechanisms. The risk model reflects this: it reaches an AUC of 0.813 and ranks accounts by cancellation probability, but it predicts *who* is likely to churn, not *why* in any causal sense, and it has not been validated out-of-time on a future window or on genuinely new accounts. The reported AUC also comes from a single 80/20 hold-out split rather than cross-validation, so with 81 accounts in the test set it carries meaningful variance. A high-risk score identifies an account worth attention; it does not guarantee that intervening will change the outcome. The model is a prioritization instrument, not proof that the levers it points to will move the result.

### Future Work

**Validate against real subscription data and recalibrate.** The natural next step is to run the same pipeline against data from a live business: recalibrate the thresholds, confirm whether the Month 1 cliff and the usage-density signal persist outside the synthetic environment, and — where the subscription architecture allows it — report NRR correctly as the primary retention metric rather than excluding it.

**Validate the model out-of-time and measure intervention lift.** Beyond the single hold-out split used here, the model should be cross-validated and validated on a forward time window to confirm it predicts churn it has not seen. The stronger extension is causal: an A/B test of the activation intervention itself — treating a random subset of new accounts with the first-30-days playbook — would move the analysis from predicting churn to measuring whether the recommended action actually reduces it.

**Deepen the activation analysis to define the real "aha moment."** The Month 1 cliff is established; what is not yet pinned down is the specific behavior that separates accounts which survive it from those which don't. Identifying the precise activation event — the action in the first weeks that best predicts long-term retention — would turn the recommended playbook from "engage early" into a concrete, measurable target, and would sharpen the early-warning signal the risk model watches for.

**Make the dashboard predictive, not just descriptive.** The current tool surfaces accounts that are already high-risk. Integrating the risk score as a live layer in the dashboard — with early-warning alerts that fire as an account's signals shift, rather than a list regenerated by hand — would move the Customer Success team from periodic manual review to continuous, preemptive monitoring, catching accounts on the way into the risk zone instead of once they arrive.
